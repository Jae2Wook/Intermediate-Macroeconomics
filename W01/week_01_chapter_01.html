Q3) The Labor Market Model:¶

Lets add some parameters to the labor market model:

\begin{align*} \text{labor supply:}\ &L^{s} = \bar{a} \times \omega + \bar{l} \\ \text{labor demand:}\ &L^{d} = \bar{f} - \omega \end{align*}

The parameters in this setup are $\bar{a},\bar{l},\text{ and }\bar{f}$. (Notice that parameters are denoted with an overbar, a convention we will maintain throughout the semester for any variable that is exogenous, or determined outside of the model.) The parameter $\bar{l}$ ) represents the number of hours workers would supply to the market even if the wage wer zero; it therefore relects the inherent amount of time peopale like to work. The parameter $\bar{f}$, in contrast, reflects the amount of labor the firm would like to hire if the wage were zero. It might be thought of as some inherent capacity of the firm (perhaps because the firm owns a given amount of land and paital that cannot be altered).

(a) What is the economic interpretation of $\bar{a}$.

elasticity of labor

(b) What are the endogenous variables in this model (variables determined within the model)?

L^{s}, L^{d}, omega

(c) Solve for the equilibrium of the labor market. That is, solve for the endogenous variables as a function of the parameters of the model.

omega = (bar{f} - bar{l}) / (bar{a} + 1)

(d) If $\bar{l}$ increases, what happens to the equilibrium wage and employment levels? Does this make sense?

equilibrium wage falls, employment level rises. Because labor supply curve shifts to the right. It makes sense.

(e) Answer the same questions in (d) for an increase in $\bar{f}$.

equilibrium wage rises, employment level rises. Because labor demand curve shifts to the right. It makes sense.
